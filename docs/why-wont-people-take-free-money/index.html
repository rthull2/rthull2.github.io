Why Won't People Take Free Money?

Robert Hull

22 Jan 2022 • 2 min read

How many people making $25/hour at their day job would be willing to spend one hour to research and sign up for a credit card in exchange for eight hours worth of pay ($200)? This can be repeated a handful of times. Of the ten people I've asked about this, only one has done it, and they've only done it once.

How many are willing to spend one hour to research, sign up, and place a risk-free bet on an online sports betting site for an expected twelve hours worth of pay ($320)? This can be repeated a couple of times. Of the ten people I've asked about this, only one has done it (the person who told me about it).

There are a few valid concerns, which I'll quickly address, but I think the true reasons go deeper.

"I don't want to lower my credit score." Yes, applying for a credit card temporarily lowers your score. This is only an issue for people looking to take out a loan in the next couple of months.

"Is this a scam, why are they giving me free money?" They make money on average, collecting lots of money from their best users. They pay you to join with the hope that you also make poor financial decisions.

There are a handful of other things you should know, such as the basics of how credit works, how you can avoid actually hurting your credit score, learning all the terminology, etc.

I think the true reason lies in the quick math people use for making decisions. For many people, researching something they're not interested in for one hour requires the same amount of mental effort they use in forty hours at their job. The math isn't so good if you're only getting $200 for forty hours worth of work.

On a similar note, why do so many people spend so little time trying to learn skills that would raise their pay? If you're going to be working full-time for the next twenty-five years at your current pay, doubling your pay either doubles the money you have or saves you 12.5 years. If it takes you one year to learn the skills that double your pay, you effectively made 12.5 years worth of money in one year. I think there's a correlation between this and the original question. If someone isn't willing to make a low cost, low reward trade, they probably won't make the high cost, high reward trade.
